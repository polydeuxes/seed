## Independent translation of Grammar A: Expression -> Warrant -> Lawful Reliance

This section translates Grammar A without reference to Grammar B.

### Constitutional participants

Grammar A's recurring participants are:

1. **Expression** — the external or presentation carrier through which bounded content becomes available to a repository surface. Expression may include provider syntax, operator prose, field names, diagnostic rows, report sections, courtroom-like labels, physiology labels, bridge labels, and other carrier forms. Expression is not itself the warrant.
2. **Warrant** — evidence-bound lawful reliance under a preserved authority boundary. Warrant is the constitutional property by which bounded content may still be lawfully relied upon after translation because evidence, provenance/support, role, authority limit, negative authority, unknowns, and confidence limits survive.
3. **Lawful reliance** — the bounded downstream entitlement to rely on content only in the role and strength authorized by repository evidence, preserved boundaries, negative authority, unknowns, and confidence limits.

### Constitutional movement

Grammar A describes a preservation movement from carrier form into bounded reliance:

```text
external expression
  carries or exposes
bounded content
  whose warrant survives only if
evidence, provenance/support, role, authority limit,
negative authority, unknowns, and confidence limits survive
  permitting only
bounded lawful reliance
```

The movement is not semantic interpretation from wording alone. It is not provider representation becoming truth. It is not an implementation mechanism. It is constitutional preservation across changing expression.

### Constitutional boundaries

Grammar A's boundaries are:

- expression may vary without changing warrant only when the same evidence-bound lawful reliance remains intact;
- expression is insufficient for warrant when it is only fluency, repetition, attractive vocabulary, provider-native excess meaning, public visibility, diagnostic availability, presentation label, operator desire, or shared wording;
- warrant is weakened or destroyed when translation changes subject identity, relation, value, agreement, unknown, inquiry condition, provenance, authority, negative authority, or confidence beyond evidence;
- unknowns, limitations, insufficiencies, candidates, frontiers, and presentation labels must not be converted into resolved truth;
- lawful reliance remains bounded and role-specific, not universal authority.

### Constitutional authority

Grammar A's authority comes from recurring repository evidence under bounded authority. Supported producers include implementation evidence, tests, diagnostics, generated architecture evidence, app behavior where implementation-facing, provider-local validated material, independent already-observed records, prior investigations and audits that preserve evidence and boundaries, and scoped family-completion evidence.

The source is not external expression by itself. Expression may carry candidate content; repository evidence and preserved authority boundaries authorize reliance.

### Preserved unknowns

Grammar A preserves these unknowns:

- whether every carrier form can preserve warrant equally;
- whether future evidence will stabilize additional warrant vocabulary;
- whether a particular expression change preserves or changes warrant when evidence is incomplete;
- whether any given downstream consumer may rely on content beyond the locally supported role;
- whether warrant in a specific case comes from implementation evidence, prior investigation evidence, provider-local evidence, or another bounded source without further review.

### Confidence

Confidence is **high** that recurring evidence supports Expression as carrier, Warrant as evidence-bound lawful reliance under preserved authority boundary, and Lawful Reliance as bounded downstream entitlement. Confidence is **low** that Grammar A names an implementation structure, universal translation mechanism, or runtime office.

## Independent translation of Grammar B: Recovered Answer -> Admissibility -> Next Constitutional Question

This section translates Grammar B without reference to Grammar A.

### Constitutional participants

Grammar B's recurring participants are:

1. **Recovered Answer** — a bounded constitutional answer or recovery that has been produced under evidence, authority boundary, negative authority, unknown preservation, confidence, and stop discipline.
2. **Admissibility** — a local evidence-bound gate that determines whether the recovered material may enter a next bounded state, such as inquiry, dispatch, projection, methodology preservation, admission, or a smallest implementation slice. In the next-question neighborhood, admissibility asks whether a next bounded evidence-bearing question or authority-bearing surface is lawfully available.
3. **Next Constitutional Question** — a bounded evidence-bearing question, exact question family, inquiry surface, audit, orientation, source lookup, reasoning path, selection path, fact-support surface, explanation, diagnostic surface, or constitutional investigation that remains inside an implemented or constitutionally evidenced boundary.

### Constitutional movement

Grammar B describes a gated movement from completed or recovered material into a further bounded inquiry only when evidence permits:

```text
recovered answer
  may pressure or expose
a candidate next question
  but movement occurs only through
local admissibility
  producing either
an admissible bounded evidence-bearing question
  or
Unknown / refusal / stop
```

The movement is not automatic next-question generation. It is not planning, curiosity, semantic routing, autonomous inquiry, or a question engine. It is bounded inquiry admissibility with lawful stop.

### Constitutional boundaries

Grammar B's boundaries are:

- a next question is admissible only when it maps to an implemented surface, exact inventory row, syntactic lookup, diagnostic/audit boundary, or recurring constitutional report;
- exact question-family behavior rejects unknown text before eligibility and dispatch;
- orientation may expose related material but does not infer intent, planning, ownership, commands, requirements, recommendations, or next safe moves;
- inquiry artifacts explicitly refuse inquiry graph creation, pressure transformation inference, workflow, planning, recording, event-ledger writing, and cluster mutation;
- missing derivation, missing selection logic, missing question-family row, missing authority boundary, unsupported ownership, diagnostic-only status, not-dispatchable status, and missing required parameters remain stop conditions or Unknowns.

### Constitutional authority

Grammar B's authority comes from local admissibility rules preserved in exact question-surface inventory, bounded ask behavior, inquiry orientation, source navigation, reasoning and selection path audits, diagnostic inventory, diagnostic shape audit, constitutional investigations, and admissibility-gate evidence.

The authority is local and gate-like. It does not arise from the recovered answer's existence alone. It requires evidence posture, authority boundary, exact identity or supported surface, unknown preservation, and stop capability.

### Preserved unknowns

Grammar B preserves these unknowns:

- whether `Admissibility` will become canonical repository vocabulary;
- whether bounded inquiry admissibility is a standalone competency or a sub-boundary of Inquiry Navigation;
- whether a stable finite set of admissible next questions exists after a recovered answer;
- whether future implementation will expose an admissibility-related diagnostic or inquiry surface;
- whether every transition family should be described with admissibility vocabulary;
- whether diagnostic question families and constitutional question investigations share a single authority boundary.

### Confidence

Confidence is **medium-high** that recurring evidence supports local admissibility gates between recovered material and a next bounded evidence-bearing question. Confidence is **low** that Grammar B names a general next-question generator, planner, semantic router, inquiry graph, runtime owner, or universal transition mechanism.

## Constitutional comparison

### Recurring constitutional participants

Both translations involve bounded material that is not self-authorizing:

- Grammar A has expression-carried bounded content that requires warrant before lawful reliance.
- Grammar B has recovered answer material that requires admissibility before lawful next-question movement.

The recurring shared participant is not the same named object. The smallest supported shared participant is **bounded evidence-bearing material under authority boundary**.

### Recurring lawful movement

Both translations describe movement only after a constitutional check:

- Grammar A moves from expression-carried content to lawful reliance only when warrant survives.
- Grammar B moves from recovered answer to next constitutional question only when admissibility permits.

The movement is therefore partially overlapping: both are evidence-bound, authority-limited, negative-authority-preserving, unknown-preserving, and stop-capable. But the direction differs. Grammar A is preservation of reliance across expression or translation. Grammar B is admission of further bounded inquiry after recovery.

### Recurring authority

Both grammars derive authority from repository evidence and preserved boundaries rather than from presentation vocabulary, repetition, desire, or recurrence alone.

The authority is not identical:

- Grammar A's authority is reliance authority: what content may be relied upon after expression changes.
- Grammar B's authority is admissibility authority: whether a further bounded question or surface may be entered.

### Recurring preservation

Both grammars preserve:

- evidence/provenance/support;
- authority boundary;
- negative authority;
- unknowns;
- confidence or evidence-strength limits;
- non-promotion boundaries;
- lawful stop.

Preservation overlaps strongly, but the preserved object differs: Grammar A preserves warrant for reliance; Grammar B preserves the recovered answer and candidate next question boundaries through admissibility.

### Recurring stop conditions

Both grammars stop where evidence or authority is missing.

Grammar A stops or weakens reliance when expression changes evidence, subject, relation, value, provenance, authority, negative authority, unknowns, or confidence beyond support.

Grammar B stops when there is no implemented or constitutionally supported surface, exact identity, required argument, dispatch boundary, derivation evidence, selection evidence, authority boundary, or admissible next-question path.

### Recurring unknowns

Both grammars preserve unknowns rather than resolving through them. The unknowns overlap at the level of evidence gaps, authority gaps, unsupported promotion, and unresolved boundary conditions. They differ in local form: Grammar A's unknowns concern preservation of reliance across expression; Grammar B's unknowns concern admissibility of next inquiry movement.

### Confidence

Confidence is **medium-high** that the two grammars partially overlap in a shared constitutional neighborhood of evidence-bound, authority-limited, unknown-preserving movement. Confidence is **medium** that they should be described as distinct local structures inside that neighborhood. Confidence is **low** that repository evidence supports identical constitutional structure or a unified bridge.

## Determination

The recovered Warrant / Reliance grammar and the recovered Question Dependency grammar describe **overlapping constitutional structures**, not an identical constitutional structure and not wholly different constitutional structures.

The smallest supported joint grammar is:

```text
bounded evidence-bearing material
↓
local constitutional authorization check
↓
bounded lawful downstream use or lawful stop
```

This joint grammar is only a comparison result. It is not a new constitutional office, canonical vocabulary, implementation plan, bridge, registry, framework, engine, or runtime architecture.

## Smallest supported distinctions

### Same constitutional participant, different constitutional movement

Both grammars handle bounded evidence-bearing material under repository authority. The movement differs:

- Grammar A: carrier expression -> preserved warrant -> lawful reliance.
- Grammar B: recovered answer -> admissibility gate -> next bounded question or stop.

### Different authority, shared preservation

Both require evidence, authority boundary, negative authority, unknown preservation, confidence calibration, and stop. But the authority differs:

- Grammar A authorizes bounded reliance on content.
- Grammar B authorizes entry into a further bounded evidence-bearing question or refuses entry.

### Shared refusal, different local stop

Both refuse promotion from presentation vocabulary, repetition, unsupported inference, or desire. But the stop is local:

- Grammar A stops reliance when warrant does not survive expression or translation.
- Grammar B stops movement when no admissible next question or supported surface exists.

### Shared non-implementation boundary

Both refuse implementation recovery. Grammar A does not recover a translation engine or warrant object. Grammar B does not recover a question engine, planner, inquiry graph, or runtime owner.
