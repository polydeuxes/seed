# Constitutional Risk / Harm Boundary Investigation

## Bounded question

Across recurring repository evidence, when Seed receives or produces a claim that something is risky, dangerous, vulnerable, exposed, sensitive, concerning, or capable of causing harm, what prevents Seed from treating that risk as proof that harm actually occurred without separately preserved event evidence, affected-subject evidence, impact evidence, temporal provenance, causal support, source authority, claim form, support path, confidence limits, Unknowns, and lawful stop?

This is exactly one bounded constitutional investigation: **Risk != Harm**. It is not world research, legal research, medical research, security evaluation, product evaluation, or implementation. It does not create a risk schema, harm schema, vulnerability engine, threat model engine, causation engine, legal-harm engine, or implementation plan.

## App-visible evidence used

The app was used only as bounded repository evidence, not as an oracle.

- `python scripts/seed_local.py --diagnostic-inventory --json` showed diagnostic surfaces with declared boundaries such as record scope, event-ledger behavior, and cluster mutation status.
- `python scripts/seed_local.py --diagnostic-shape-audit --json` showed that diagnostic-shape auditing checks declared/observed diagnostic behavior.
- `python scripts/seed_local.py --knowledge-reachability-audit --knowledge-reachability-audit-json --knowledge-reachability-audit-limit 20` showed reachability as a bounded audit over preserved/projected/read-model/inquiry/rendered stages, with candidate limits and truncation metadata.
- `python scripts/seed_local.py --reasoning-path pressure capability` showed a read-only derivation explanation surface. For the reviewed subject/domain it reported no observed evidence, no intermediate conclusions, no derived conclusions, and an explicit boundary: no recording, event-ledger writes, or cluster mutation.
- `python scripts/seed_local.py --selection-path current_focus` showed a read-only selection explanation surface. It exposed selected/non-selected pressure candidates and the ordering rule, then stopped at a no-recording/no-mutation boundary.

These outputs support only the existence and behavior of bounded app-visible surfaces. They do not prove any public-world risk, harm, vulnerability, exploit, injury, legal violation, misuse, or causal chain.

## Reviewed repository evidence

The investigation reviewed recurring repository evidence around:

- capability and use boundaries, especially capability candidates, capability inventory, verification, policy risk class, tool need, and action-plan risk fields;
- public evidence packet readiness and external artifact intake boundaries;
- external source role, source independence, and authority/volume boundaries;
- absence-of-evidence, model-output/evidence, translation/rendering, repetition/proof, narrative/history, and boundary-mismatch/invalidity investigations;
- observation, evidence, facts, fact support, source navigation, knowledge reachability, reasoning path, and selection path implementations;
- diagnostic inventory and diagnostic shape-audit implementations;
- warrant, lawful reliance, lawful acceptance, claim form, temporal provenance, event/history/provenance boundaries, confidence/evidence strength, Unknown preservation, typed Unknowns, and implementation-pressure boundaries.

The most relevant recurring repository pattern is non-promotion: visible material may become bounded evidence for a bounded claim only when its support, provenance, claim form, authority, confidence, Unknowns, and stop conditions survive. Otherwise, the lawful result is unsupported, Unknown, investigation pressure, or stop.

## Risk analysis

Repository evidence does not appear to define a general constitutional `risk` ontology. It does preserve several narrower risk-like uses:

1. tool/action `risk_class` and policy-gate behavior;
2. recommendation ranking that can prefer lower-risk classes;
3. pressure or concern language that may orient attention;
4. diagnostic findings that may identify conditions deserving inspection;
5. documents warning that risky, possible, or available conditions do not authorize action by themselves.

Therefore, for this bounded investigation, `risk`, `hazard`, `danger`, `possibility`, `threat`, `concern`, `sensitivity`, `unsafe design`, and `potential harm` are best treated as claim forms or investigation pressure unless a more specific repository owner supplies a narrower meaning. A risk claim may say that a condition, design, capability, exposure, or possibility was reported or observed within a bounded system/context/time window. It does not itself say that harm occurred.

## Harm analysis

Repository evidence also does not appear to define a general constitutional `harm` ontology. The absence-of-evidence boundary explicitly treats missing harm evidence as not proving no harm occurred, which implies the repository recognizes harm as a possible claim type while refusing to adjudicate it from missing local support alone. Adjacent evidence separates event history, causation, source authority, legal authority, affected subject, diagnostic output, and implementation pressure.

For this investigation, `actual harm`, `injury`, `damage`, `rights impact`, `misuse`, `affected subject`, `legal harm`, and `proof of harm` are not interchangeable. They require separately preserved support appropriate to the claim form. In particular, legal harm requires legal authority support; causation requires causal support; affected-subject claims require affected-subject evidence; exploit occurrence requires event or occurrence evidence; proof of conduct requires conduct evidence rather than harm narrative alone.

## Risk / occurrence distinction

`Risk of X` is a bounded claim that X could occur, may occur, is more likely under some condition, or has a reported vulnerability/concern/exposure path. `X occurred` is an event/history claim. The separator is event evidence plus temporal provenance, affected context, source/support path, claim form, and confidence. Without occurrence evidence, risk of X remains possible/probable/concern evidence, not occurrence.

## Vulnerability / exploit-occurrence distinction

A vulnerability claim says a weakness, exposure, unsafe condition, or exploitability path may exist. Exploit occurrence says someone or something actually exploited it in a time, context, and event sequence. The former can be supported by configuration, design, code, advisory, or source-report evidence. The latter requires occurrence evidence: event logs, incident records, preserved observations, source authority for the event claim, temporal boundaries, affected subject/system evidence, and causal/support path. Vulnerability is not exploit occurrence.

## Exposure / injury distinction

Exposure may mean a subject, data class, endpoint, system, or condition was accessible, visible, disclosed, reachable, or placed at risk. Injury means an affected subject or interest was actually harmed. Exposure can lawfully support `exposure existed` only when source material, scope, system/context, time window, and claim form are preserved. Injury requires affected-subject and impact evidence. Exposure is not injury.

## Concern / affected-subject-evidence distinction

Concern is an attention or assessment claim. It may be operator concern, source concern, diagnostic concern, policy concern, privacy concern, security concern, or safety concern. Affected-subject evidence identifies who or what was affected and how. Repository evidence repeatedly refuses to treat concern, orientation, pressure, or related material as fact, goal, action, command, or next safe move. Concern is not affected-subject evidence.

## Unsafe-design / actual-damage distinction

Unsafe design is a design-condition or evaluation claim. It may identify a hazardous architecture, weak control, missing guardrail, risky default, or dangerous capability. Actual damage is an impact/event claim. Unsafe design may support bounded design-risk claims if design evidence and authority are preserved. It does not prove that damage occurred, that any subject was affected, or that a later event was caused by the design.

## Alleged-harm / proven-harm distinction

Alleged harm is a claim reported by a source or actor. Proven harm is a repository-lawful conclusion supported within a bounded evidentiary and authority path. The boundary requires preserved source role, source material, support path, independent support where needed, event/impact/affected-subject evidence, claim form, confidence limits, Unknowns, and authority to make the conclusion. Allegation is not proof.

## Possible-harm / probable-harm distinction

Possible harm means a harm scenario is not ruled out or could occur under some conditions. Probable harm is a stronger likelihood claim and requires evidence supporting probability, not merely possibility vocabulary. Repository evidence supports preserving confidence/evidence strength limits. Without a repository-supported probability basis, Seed may not promote possible harm to probable harm.

## Probable-harm / actual-harm distinction

Probable harm remains a likelihood or forecast claim. Actual harm is an occurrence/impact claim. Even strong probability does not prove occurrence without event, affected-subject, and impact evidence. The lawful boundary is: probability can support bounded likelihood claims; occurrence requires preserved occurrence support.

## Actual-harm / legal-harm distinction

Actual harm is a factual/impact claim. Legal harm is a legal-authority claim: it depends on recognized legal authority, jurisdiction, elements, standing/rights concepts where applicable, and a lawful interpretation boundary. Repository evidence around legal authority and constitutional warrant prevents Seed from treating factual harm as legal harm without authority support. Actual harm is not legal harm.

## Harm / causation distinction

Harm says an injury, damage, or impact occurred. Causation says a specific condition, actor, system, event, vulnerability, risk, design, or use caused that harm. Repository event models preserve causation identifiers for event lineage, and history surfaces explicitly warn that correlation may be visible while causation is not proven. Harm is not causation.

## Harm-evidence / proof-of-conduct distinction

Harm evidence may show impact. Proof of conduct shows that a specific actor/system performed specific conduct. A subject can be harmed without the repository proving who caused it, what conduct occurred, or whether conduct was lawful. Proof of conduct requires conduct/event/source evidence, not harm evidence alone.

## Sensitivity / demonstrated-impact distinction

Privacy, security, safety, legal, medical, operational, or policy sensitivity identifies a class of material or context requiring care. Demonstrated impact identifies what happened and how it affected a subject/system/interest. Sensitivity can lawfully justify caution, non-promotion, bounded handling, or stop. It does not demonstrate impact.

## Source-reported-harm / independently-supported-harm distinction

Source-reported harm preserves that a source made a harm claim. Independently supported harm requires meaningful support-path separation rather than repetition, syndication, translation, rendering, or one source family repeated across carriers. External source role and source-independence evidence prevent Seed from treating source volume as independent proof.

## Repeated-harm-narrative / evidence-of-harm distinction

Repeated harm narratives may show recurrence of a claim, story, allegation, or framing. Repetition/proof and narrative/history boundaries prevent repetition from becoming proof. Evidence of harm must preserve support for event, impact, affected subject, source authority, temporal provenance, claim form, confidence, and Unknown limits. Repeated narrative is not harm evidence unless repetition itself is the bounded claim.

## Affected-subject evidence analysis

Affected-subject evidence answers who or what was affected. It must preserve subject identity or bounded anonymized subject class, source/support path, time/context, and the claimed effect. Without it, Seed may say at most that a risk, concern, alleged harm, or impact claim was reported or observed, not that a person/entity/system was affected.

## Event-evidence analysis

Event evidence answers what happened. It requires preserved event material, timestamp or time window, subject/context, source, provenance, and claim form. Risk, hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and design weakness are not event evidence unless the event claim is specifically that the risk/exposure/vulnerability/design condition was observed at a time.

## Impact-evidence analysis

Impact evidence answers what changed or was damaged/injured. It must preserve impact type, affected subject/context, source, support path, temporal boundary, confidence, and Unknowns. Impact evidence is separate from event evidence because an event can occur without demonstrated impact, and impact can be alleged without a supported causal event.

## Temporal-provenance analysis

Temporal provenance bounds when a risk, exposure, vulnerability, event, harm, or impact claim is asserted. It prevents stale, current, possible, historical, and future claims from collapsing. Before Seed says `risk X existed in this system/context/time window`, it must preserve the reviewed time window, source time, observation time, and any freshness/staleness limits. Before Seed says `harm X occurred`, it must preserve occurrence timing or a bounded Unknown about timing.

## Causal-support analysis

Causal support links condition/event/conduct to impact. It requires more than temporal order, correlation, source assertion, repeated narrative, or risk vocabulary. It must preserve the alleged cause, alleged effect, mechanism or event chain where available, support path, source authority, confidence, Unknowns, and alternative/unsupported paths. Without causal support, lawful stop prevents `risk X caused harm Y`.

## Claim-form analysis

Claim form is central. Seed must distinguish at least these forms:

- `source S alleged harm X`;
- `risk X was reported`;
- `risk X existed in context C during time T`;
- `harm X was alleged`;
- `harm X occurred`;
- `subject A was affected by impact I`;
- `vulnerability V existed`;
- `exploit E occurred`;
- `risk X caused harm Y`;
- `harm X was legal harm`;
- `evidence is insufficient / Unknown`.

The same words can appear in different claim forms. Lawful reliance depends on the preserved claim form, not on vocabulary alone.

## Confidence / evidence-strength analysis

Confidence and evidence strength prevent overclaiming. Possible, probable, alleged, source-reported, independently supported, proven, unknown, stale, unsupported, and insufficient are different strengths. Repository evidence supports confidence limits as boundaries on movement. Seed may not strengthen a claim by rhetorical force, source volume, repetition, model output, translation, or operator concern.

## Unknown preservation

Unknown preservation is the positive lawful result when support, authority, source independence, event evidence, affected-subject evidence, impact evidence, temporal provenance, causal support, legal authority, or implementation evidence is missing. Typed Unknown evidence reinforces that unresolved investigation state can be preserved without resolving it. Unknown is not failure, falsity, no-harm, no-risk, no-event, no-authority, no-causation, or implementation permission.

## Role of required evidence classes

- **Affected-subject evidence** prevents concern/exposure/risk from becoming a claim that a person, entity, right, data class, service, or system was affected.
- **Event evidence** prevents risk/vulnerability/design weakness from becoming occurrence.
- **Impact evidence** prevents occurrence/exposure from becoming injury or damage.
- **Temporal provenance** prevents stale/current/future/possible/historical claims from collapsing.
- **Causal support** prevents harm from becoming caused-by-this-risk.
- **Source authority** prevents any source, model, translation, repetition, or volume from becoming authority beyond its role.
- **Claim form** prevents allegation, possibility, probability, occurrence, legal conclusion, and implementation recommendation from collapsing.
- **Support path** preserves how the claim is supported and whether paths are independent.
- **Confidence limits** prevent possible/probable/source-reported claims from becoming proven or actual.
- **Unknown preservation** provides the lawful non-promotion result.

## Conditions for lawful bounded risk-related claims

Risk evidence can lawfully support a bounded harm-related claim only when Seed states the smaller claim it actually supports. Examples:

- `source S reported risk X`;
- `surface A exposed risk-related finding X within scope C at time T`;
- `risk X was preserved as an unresolved concern`;
- `risk X may support investigation pressure`;
- `risk X is relevant to a later warranted harm inquiry`;
- `harm evidence remains Unknown`.

Risk evidence may not lawfully support `harm occurred`, `injury occurred`, `risk caused harm`, `legal harm exists`, `proof of conduct exists`, or `implementation work is required` unless the separate evidence and authority for those stronger claims are preserved.

## Before Seed may say `risk X existed in this system/context/time window`

Seed must preserve:

1. the risk claim form and vocabulary actually used;
2. source material or app-visible observation supporting the risk;
3. system/context boundaries;
4. time window, observation time, and source time/freshness limits;
5. whether the claim is reported, observed, inferred, diagnostic, policy, design, vulnerability, exposure, concern, or sensitivity;
6. support path and source role/authority;
7. confidence/evidence strength;
8. Unknowns and non-promotion limits.

## Before Seed may say `harm X occurred`

Seed must preserve:

1. event or occurrence evidence for the harm;
2. affected-subject evidence or a bounded subject Unknown;
3. impact/injury/damage evidence;
4. temporal provenance;
5. source material, source role, and support path;
6. claim form distinguishing actual harm from alleged, possible, probable, legal, or caused harm;
7. confidence/evidence strength;
8. Unknowns and lawful stop limits.

## Before Seed may say `risk X caused harm Y`

Seed must preserve:

1. bounded support that risk X existed;
2. bounded support that harm Y occurred;
3. causal support linking X to Y;
4. event sequence or mechanism where required by the claim form;
5. affected-subject and impact evidence;
6. temporal provenance sufficient for the causal claim;
7. source authority and independent support where needed;
8. confidence limits, alternative/unsupported paths, and Unknowns;
9. authority to make a causation claim rather than merely report an allegation.

## Lawful stop

The lawful stop condition is reached whenever Seed has only risk, hazard, danger, possibility, exposure, vulnerability, threat, concern, sensitivity, unsafe-design, potential-harm, probable-harm, or alleged-harm material without the separately preserved evidence and authority required for occurrence, injury, damage, affected-subject, exploit occurrence, misuse, causation, legal harm, proof of conduct, or implementation pressure.

At that point Seed must preserve the bounded claim and stop at one of: reported risk, observed risk, alleged harm, possible harm, probable harm, concern, sensitivity, insufficient evidence, unsupported conclusion, Unknown, or investigation pressure. It must not promote the material into actual harm, causation, legal harm, proof of conduct, or implementation work.

## Supported conclusions

1. Repository evidence supports the negative constitutional boundary: **Risk != Harm**.
2. Risk vocabulary is currently best treated as bounded claim form or investigation pressure unless a specific repository owner supplies a narrower implemented meaning.
3. Harm vocabulary is not a single repository-wide ontology; actual harm, injury, damage, legal harm, affected subject, causation, exploit occurrence, misuse, proof of conduct, and implementation pressure require distinct support.
4. Vulnerability is not exploit occurrence.
5. Exposure is not injury.
6. Concern is not affected-subject evidence.
7. Unsafe design is not actual damage.
8. Alleged harm is not proven harm.
9. Possible harm is not probable harm without probability support.
10. Probable harm is not actual harm without occurrence/impact support.
11. Actual harm is not legal harm without legal authority support.
12. Harm is not causation.
13. Harm evidence is not proof of conduct.
14. Sensitivity is not demonstrated impact.
15. Source-reported harm is not independently supported harm.
16. Repeated harm narrative is not evidence of harm unless the bounded claim is only that the narrative repeated.
17. Unknown preservation and lawful stop are the core brakes that prevent risk material from becoming stronger conclusions.

## Unsupported conclusions

The reviewed evidence does not support concluding that:

- a general risk schema exists;
- a general harm schema exists;
- Seed has a vulnerability, exploit, threat-model, harm-evaluation, legal-harm, privacy-impact, security-evaluation, or causation engine;
- any real public-world risk or harm occurred;
- any real vulnerability was exploited;
- any exposure injured anyone;
- any concern identifies an affected subject;
- any unsafe design caused damage;
- any alleged harm is proven;
- any possible/probable harm is actual harm;
- any actual harm is legal harm;
- any risk caused any harm;
- any harm evidence proves conduct;
- any repeated narrative proves harm;
- risk/harm vocabulary should be promoted into ontology;
- implementation work is required.

## Implementation recommendation

No implementation work is recommended. This investigation found no concrete failing command, behavior, diagnostic visibility gap, or repository authority requiring implementation. The supported result is a constitutional boundary discipline: preserve risk/harm claim form, source material, provenance, event evidence, affected-subject evidence, impact evidence, temporal boundary, causal support, authority boundary, support path, confidence limits, Unknowns, and lawful stop before making stronger harm, causation, legal-harm, proof-of-conduct, or implementation-pressure claims.
