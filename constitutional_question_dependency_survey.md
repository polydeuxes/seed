## Independent testimony from recurring investigation sequences

Each sequence testifies independently here. Comparison is deferred until the next section.

### Sequence 1: Scout -> Survey

**What question was asked?**

Whether visible terrain, pressure, landmarks, or candidate recurrence exist without characterization, family recovery, topology, or implementation recommendation; then, at survey resolution, what local terrain or neighborhood is visible without recovering ownership, bridges, topology, competencies, or implementation work.

**Why could it be asked?**

The survey question could be asked because prior Scout work supplied a bounded search warrant: visible recurrence, landmarks, pressure, preserved unknowns, and confidence sufficient for local observational resolution, not sufficient for characterization or recovery.

**Which earlier question(s) made it admissible?**

The Scout question made the Survey question admissible by establishing that there was a bounded observed terrain worth surveying. The earlier question was: what is visible without stronger recovery?

**Which questions were explicitly not yet admissible?**

Characterization, competency recovery, district recovery, topology recovery, bridge recovery, ownership recovery, runtime recovery, implementation recommendation, and slice recommendation remained not yet admissible at Scout or Survey resolution unless later evidence supplied a stronger warrant.

**Confidence.** High for this dependency as recovery methodology. Low for constitutional topology.

**Why could it be asked?**

**Confidence.** High for recurring methodological dependency. Medium-low for any claim that Characterization is constitutionally downstream of Survey in every case.

## Comparison

### Recurring question dependencies

Recurring repository evidence supports dependency-shaped investigation orderings:

1. **Visible terrain question -> local survey question.** A Scout-style question about visible recurrence commonly makes a Survey-style question admissible.
2. **Surveyed evidence question -> characterization question.** A Characterization commonly depends on prior bounded evidence, surveyed terrain, or investigated scope.
3. **Identity/scope question -> evidence-specific inquiry.** Bounded inquiry repeatedly depends on exact subject, question family, target, query, domain, note, required arguments, or authority boundary.
4. **Frontier/admissibility question -> later bounded constitutional question.** Frontier surveys and prioritization admit later questions by preserving recurring pressure and excluding unsupported frames.
5. **Evidence/readiness question -> proceed/stop question.** Readiness depends on earlier evidence of compression, fulfilled upstream work, ownership/authority boundaries, compatibility, and stop criteria.
6. **Endpoint identity question -> bridge/topology relation question.** Bridge or topology questions require visible bounded sides before a relation can be tested.
7. **Authority/stop question -> competency or transition conclusion.** Competency and transition investigations depend on prior evidence of boundary, negative authority, unknown preservation, and stop discipline.

These are `Question -> Question` dependencies in the limited sense that a later question becomes lawfully askable only after an earlier question has supplied bounded identity, evidence, scope, support, authority, or stop conditions.

### Recurring admissibility dependencies

The recurring admissibility dependencies are:

- exact question or surface identity before answering;
- required arguments or bounded target before evidence selection;
- evidence/support source before characterization or promotion;
- authority boundary before movement, reliance, or conclusion;
- Unknown/refusal/stop status before generating or admitting another question;
- compatibility and non-mutation boundaries before operational or diagnostic claims;
- prior recovery or survey evidence before higher-resolution characterization;
- local family evidence before family-local transition-gate conclusions.

### Recurring stop conditions

Later questions stop or remain not yet admissible when:

- the candidate is vocabulary-only, presentation-only, metaphor-only, or prose pressure;
- exact identity, target, or required arguments are absent;
- evidence is insufficient, unsupported, or belongs to another local family;
- the next step would require ownership, topology, implementation, runtime behavior, scheduler/planner behavior, mutation, execution, truth creation, or autonomous question generation;
- bridge/topology pressure lacks bounded endpoints;
- readiness evidence points to stop or insufficient implementation evidence;
- confidence is too low or unknowns remain material.

### Recurring prerequisite questions

The recurring prerequisite questions are not one canonical list, but they have stable shapes:

- What is the bounded subject, surface, family, target, query, or note?
- What evidence has already been observed?
- What local authority permits this question?
- What is explicitly outside authority?
- What unknowns or stop conditions remain?
- What prior Scout, Survey, Frontier, Readiness, or Resolution artifact supplied the warrant?
- What relation, if any, is testable without topology or implementation recovery?

### Preserved unknowns

This survey preserves these unknowns:

- Whether any subset of investigation dependencies belongs to constitutional reality rather than recovery methodology.
- Whether specific question dependencies are stable across all constitutional domains or only across reviewed investigations.
- Whether `Question -> Question` dependency should ever be promoted into implemented public inventory, diagnostic shape, or constitutional answer family.
- Whether recurring methodology dependencies later reveal additional family-local transition gates.
- Whether any topology or bridge dependency exists beyond bounded endpoint-before-relation discipline.
- Whether the order `Resolution -> Topology -> Characterization` is a constitutional ordering, a common recovery sequence, or a compression of several local sequences.

### Confidence

**High confidence** that recurring repository evidence supports dependencies between constitutional questions at the level of investigation admissibility: later questions repeatedly depend on earlier bounded identity, evidence, scope, authority, readiness, endpoint, or stop questions.

**Medium confidence** that some dependencies touch constitutional reality, because admissibility, authority, lawful movement, Unknown preservation, and family-local transition gates are constitutional evidence, not merely writing style.

**Low confidence** that investigation dependency itself has been recovered as constitutional topology, an office, a model, a planner, a scheduler, or a runtime architecture. The reviewed evidence repeatedly forbids those promotions.

## Final answer

According to recurring repository evidence, recurring question dependencies are part of both constitutional reality and the recovery methodology, with an important boundary: the recurring dependency **as investigation order and admissibility discipline** is strongly supported in the recovery methodology, while only the locally evidenced prerequisites themselves--identity, evidence, authority, admissibility, Unknown preservation, readiness, lawful movement, and stop--are supported as constitutional reality. The repository does not support promoting investigation dependency into constitutional topology, implementation, runtime architecture, new ownership, or a universal transition model.

Question dependency survey complete.
