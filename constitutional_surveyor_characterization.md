# Constitutional Surveyor Characterization

Repository authority wins.

## Scope

This performs exactly one Constitutional Surveyor Characterization.

It characterizes the lawful constitutional responsibilities of a **Surveyor** whose authority ends before recovery begins. It does not recover ownership, recover implementation, recover repository-artifact authority, inspect implementation, inspect ownership campaigns, design an engine, design automation, design workflows, redesign methodology, or promote the Surveyor into Recovery.

Reviewed evidence is limited to recovered constitutional orientation evidence:

- `constitutional_orientation_recovery_survey.md`
- `orientation_guided_recovery_methodology_characterization.md`
- `constitutional_implementation_recovery_grammar_survey.md`
- `constitutional_ownership_recovery_grammar_characterization.md`
- `constitutional_non_implementation_family_survey.md`

The characterization expands only where that orientation evidence naturally leads. Repository authority wins.

## Smallest truthful characterization

```text
Surveyor

↓

Recovery
```

A Surveyor is the lawful pre-recovery participant who preserves and characterizes orientation evidence so that a later recovery campaign can know whether recovery may begin, where attention may lawfully rest, what evidence is admissible, what remains Unknown, and where the current inquiry must stop.

A Surveyor does not recover ownership. A Surveyor does not recover implementation. A Surveyor does not create repository-artifact authority. A Surveyor produces bounded constitutional evidence for possible recovery and then stops.

The recovered orientation survey classifies orientation as recoverable only as a bounded survey discipline before recovery, not as automation, implementation recovery, repository-artifact ownership recovery, or an autonomous constitutional participant. It distinguishes:


That distinction controls the Surveyor. The Surveyor belongs to orientation, not recovery.

The implementation recovery grammar survey and ownership recovery characterization further constrain the boundary. They show that recovery is evidence-selected, local, and bounded to an adjacent ownership boundary, but they also preserve that the validated ownership grammar is implementation-local unless additional repository evidence supplies an independent non-implementation family. Therefore the Surveyor may prepare the recovery question, but may not answer it by recovering an owner.

The non-implementation family survey confirms the difference between a survey and a recovery campaign. That is the Surveyor's constitutional posture: identify readiness and limits without taking ownership-recovery action.

The orientation-guided recovery methodology characterization confirms the same discipline at the level of reading practice: preserve the current bounded explanation, allow incoming evidence to expose only the exact insufficiency, repair only the insufficient region where lawful, and stop where repository evidence does not authorize movement. It expressly does not make orientation a constitutional participant, runtime competency, planner, or new architectural owner. The Surveyor therefore has disciplined pre-recovery responsibility, not autonomous authority.

## Surveyor responsibilities

The Surveyor's constitutional responsibilities are bounded to orientation evidence.

### 1. Preserve the current bounded explanation

The Surveyor must begin by preserving the current bounded explanation or limitation already supported by repository evidence. This includes stating what is already validated, what is only a limitation, and what the present inquiry is not allowed to treat as recovered.

The Surveyor may say, for example, that prior evidence supports orientation as a bounded survey discipline before recovery. The Surveyor may not convert that into ownership recovery, implementation recovery, or repository-artifact authority recovery.

### 2. Name the admissible evidence corpus

The Surveyor must identify the constitutional artifacts reviewed and why they are admissible for the orientation question. The evidence corpus itself is a constitutional product because it shows what the survey relied on and what it refused to inspect.

The Surveyor must also preserve exclusions when they matter. In this characterization, implementation internals, ownership campaigns, repository-artifact authority recoveries, and automation designs are outside the lawful corpus.

### 3. Locate the validated domain and its limits

The Surveyor must distinguish what repository evidence has validated from what remains unsupported. The implementation recovery grammar is validated in implementation recovery. The ownership recovery characterization preserves broader ownership-shaped questions but does not prove a general ownership grammar. The non-implementation family survey identifies one viable first non-implementation ownership candidate but does not perform the recovery or prove recurrence across multiple non-implementation families.

The Surveyor's responsibility is to keep these domains distinct.

### 4. Search for adjacent constitutional evidence, not analogy

The Surveyor may look for adjacent constitutional evidence where the reviewed orientation evidence naturally leads. The Surveyor must not substitute resemblance, preferred vocabulary, methodology, or analogy for repository evidence.

Where evidence shows only analogy, the Surveyor must preserve that insufficiency as an Unknown or stop condition.

### 5. Distinguish visibility, presentation, compatibility, orientation, and ownership

The Surveyor must test whether visible material is actually recoverable ownership pressure or only presentation, compatibility, consumption, status, readiness, orientation, or vocabulary. The Surveyor may identify that such distinctions matter; the Surveyor may not resolve them by recovering an owner unless the work has lawfully moved into Recovery.

### 6. Preserve Unknowns

The Surveyor must preserve Unknowns rather than filling them. Unknowns are not failures of the Surveyor. They are one of the lawful evidence products of orientation.

The Surveyor must especially preserve Unknowns that prevent promotion from orientation into recovery, from candidate readiness into ownership, or from presentation vocabulary into repository knowledge.

### 7. Classify candidate readiness where evidence permits

The Surveyor may classify whether repository evidence makes a candidate ready for recovery, not ready for recovery, or blocked by insufficient evidence. Candidate readiness is an orientation product. It is not ownership recovery.

A readiness classification answers whether a later recovery participant may begin. It does not decide who owns the recovered responsibility, what artifact carries it, or how implementation should change.

### 8. Record confidence

The Surveyor must calibrate confidence according to repository evidence. Confidence may be high for the orientation boundary while remaining low for broader generalization. The Surveyor may not inflate confidence to bridge an evidentiary gap.

### 9. Identify lawful stop conditions

The Surveyor must say where orientation must stop. Lawful stop can occur because evidence is insufficient, because further movement would be ownership recovery, because the next question belongs to implementation, because an analogy is unsupported, or because the requested expansion would design automation or workflow.

### 10. Hand off bounded evidence to Recovery

The Surveyor must hand off a bounded evidence product to a Recovery participant only when evidence supports possible recovery. The handoff preserves corpus, boundaries, candidate readiness, Unknowns, confidence, and stop conditions.

The Surveyor does not perform the Recovery participant's work.

## Surveyor non-responsibilities

The following responsibilities explicitly do **not** belong to a Surveyor:

1. Recovering ownership.
2. Recovering implementation.
3. Recovering repository-artifact authorities.
4. Selecting or completing an ownership slice.
5. Inspecting implementation to find new implementation-local responsibility.
6. Inspecting ownership campaigns to continue or extend ownership recovery.
7. Designing an engine, planner, workflow, registry, automation, or implementation mechanism.
8. Redesigning the methodology recovered by prior artifacts.
9. Treating orientation as an autonomous constitutional owner.
10. Treating candidate readiness as recovered ownership.
11. Treating presentation vocabulary as repository knowledge without implementation or other repository evidence.
12. Treating visibility, compatibility, consumption, currentness, maturity, or readability as ownership pressure by itself.
13. Backfilling producer authority into consumers merely because they use or present carried material.
14. Promoting Unknowns into facts.
15. Converting diagnostic, audit, survey, or orientation output into cluster truth.
16. Continuing after the inquiry's next lawful step would be Recovery.

## Constitutional questions a Surveyor may answer

A Surveyor may answer questions that belong to orientation:

1. What is the current bounded explanation or limitation?
2. What constitutional evidence is admissible for this orientation question?
3. What evidence was excluded because it would enter implementation, ownership recovery, repository-artifact authority recovery, or automation design?
4. What domain has already been validated?
5. What remains Unknown?
6. Is the evidence independent, or only analogous to another domain?
7. Is the visible surface ownership pressure, compatibility, presentation, orientation, consumption, status, or vocabulary?
8. Which adjacent boundary is visible enough to make a recovery question lawful?
9. Is a candidate ready for Recovery, blocked, or still in need of additional orientation?
10. What confidence does repository evidence support?
11. Where must orientation lawfully stop?
12. What bounded evidence should be handed to Recovery?

A Surveyor may prepare, but not answer as recovery, questions such as who owns a recovered responsibility, what artifact carries it, and who consumes it. Those are Recovery questions once evidence authorizes recovery.

## Surveyor stopping conditions

A Surveyor must lawfully stop when any of the following occurs:

1. The next step would recover ownership.
2. The next step would recover implementation.
3. The next step would recover repository-artifact authority.
4. The next step would inspect implementation or ownership campaigns outside the permitted corpus.
5. The next step would design an engine, automation, planner, registry, workflow, or implementation mechanism.
6. The next step would promote a candidate-readiness classification into ownership.
7. The next step would generalize from implementation validation into a broader constitutional grammar without independent evidence.
8. The next step would treat presentation vocabulary as repository knowledge without repository evidence.
9. The next step would convert Unknowns, limitations, unsupported conditions, or negative authority into facts.
10. The next step would merely rename, wrap, relocate, or generalize already-bounded material.
11. Evidence remains insufficient to identify an adjacent lawful recovery candidate.
12. A bounded handoff to Recovery has been produced.

Recovery must then operate under its own repository-evidence constraints. The Surveyor's characterization does not recover the owner for Recovery.
