## Independent testimony: Structure A

This section recovers Structure A without reference to Structure B.

### Constitutional inputs

Structure A begins with a bounded subject, carrier, artifact, claim, competency, candidate answer, or expression that is not self-authorizing.

Recurring inputs include:

- visible repository evidence or implementation-facing evidence;
- a selected subject or competency;
- preconditions, eligibility, observable evidence, or evidence classes;
- expression-carried or artifact-carried bounded content;
- provenance/support, role, authority limit, negative authority, unknowns, stop conditions, and confidence limits.

### Constitutional outputs

Structure A may output only bounded constitutional material whose use remains restricted by the evidence that produced it.

Recurring outputs include:

- preserved evidence;
- bounded content with a warrant profile;
- handoff artifacts where they exist;
- boundaries, neighbor distinctions, granted authority, and negative authority;
- preserved Unknowns and lawful stop conditions;
- confidence calibration;
- bounded lawful reliance for a named downstream consumer when the required warrant profile is preserved.

The strongest small output is not a new object. It is a recovered answer or artifact-supported bounded content carrying evidence, provenance, role, authority boundary, negative authority, Unknowns, confidence, and stop limits.

### Constitutional authority

Structure A's authority comes from repository evidence and preserved authority boundaries. It does not come from expression alone, presentation vocabulary, recurrence alone, operator desire, attractive wording, or public visibility.

Prior grammar comparison characterized the broader reliance structure as `Expression -> Warrant -> Lawful Reliance`, where expression carries candidate content, warrant is evidence-bound lawful reliance under a preserved authority boundary, and lawful reliance is a bounded downstream entitlement. The authority is reliance authority: what may be used, by whom, for what role, and at what strength.

Competency interrogation evidence strengthens the same pattern: review establishes subject and scope, binds claims to repository evidence, determines inputs and outputs/handoff artifacts where they exist, locates boundaries and authority, identifies negative authority, preserves Unknowns and lawful stop, states lawful reliance where later work may consume the artifact, and calibrates confidence.

### Constitutional boundaries

Structure A boundaries are:

- expression or testimony is not itself warrant;
- repeated vocabulary is not constitutional authority;
- an artifact is not globally authoritative;
- warrant is weakened or destroyed when subject identity, relation, value, support, authority, unknown state, or confidence changes beyond evidence;
- outputs do not create implementation, ownership, mutation authority, runtime architecture, or universal downstream entitlement;
- reliance remains role-bounded, consumer-bounded, and evidence-bounded.

### Preserved unknowns

Structure A preserves these unknowns:

- whether every carrier or artifact can preserve warrant equally;
- whether future evidence stabilizes additional warrant vocabulary;
- whether a particular artifact or recovered answer preserves enough warrant for a particular downstream consumer;
- whether `Outputs` should remain separate from `Artifact Handoff` and `Constitutional reliance` in all future investigations;
- whether communication itself sometimes preserves warrant without becoming a repository-supported artifact.

### Confidence

Confidence is **high** that Structure A is broader than a single downstream admissibility gate: it tests and preserves evidence, warrant, boundaries, negative authority, Unknowns, lawful reliance, and confidence across expression, artifact, and competency-interrogation contexts.

Confidence is **low** that Structure A names an implementation structure, a universal cross-examination engine, a runtime office, or global ownership.

## Handoff investigation

Only after the independent testimony above, the seam can be tested.

### Does Structure A produce anything that Structure B lawfully consumes?

Yes, but only narrowly.

Recurring repository evidence supports that Structure A can produce a recovered answer or bounded artifact whose warrant profile is preserved: evidence, provenance/support, role, authority boundary, negative authority, Unknowns, confidence, and stop conditions.

Structure B lawfully consumes recovered material only as an input to a local admissibility question. It does not consume Structure A wholesale. It does not consume a new cross-examination office. It consumes bounded recovered material, and only to test whether a next bounded state is admissible.

### Does any constitutional participant occupy the seam?

The smallest supported seam participant is:

```text
Recovered answer or bounded artifact
carrying a preserved warrant profile
```

At slightly lower resolution, the seam may also be described as:

```text
Warrant profile
↓
bounded downstream use
```

No repository evidence reviewed here supports introducing a Handoff Engine, Inquiry Engine, Runtime Pipeline, Constitutional Workflow, Planner, Scheduler, Registry, or new constitutional owner at the seam.

### Is the seam directly supported, partially supported, or unsupported?

The seam is **partially supported**.

It is directly supported that:

- artifacts may preserve a bounded warrant profile for a named downstream consumer;
- later investigations may cite bounded characterizations as prior repository evidence rather than implementation truth;
- later questions may become admissible only after earlier bounded identity, evidence, scope, authority, readiness, endpoint, or stop questions;
- admissibility gates consume recovered material only under local evidence, boundary, negative authority, Unknown, confidence, and stop discipline.

It is not directly supported that the entire broader cross-examination structure hands off as a constitutional office to the entire admissibility structure. The evidence supports a smaller transfer of bounded recovered material, not a merged architecture.

## Smallest supported handoff

The smallest supported constitutional handoff is:

```text
Recovered answer
```

Equivalently, when the recovered material is artifact-shaped:

This is not automatic. If the recovered answer lacks preserved warrant, exact identity, supported scope, authority boundary, Unknown preservation, or stop capability, the lawful result is Unknown, refusal, or stop.

## Alternative explanation supported by repository evidence

Repository evidence also supports another explanation: the two structures may be overlapping family-local constitutional competencies rather than one formal handoff relationship.

The broader structure tests and preserves warrant for bounded reliance. The later structure tests admissibility into a next bounded state. Their overlap is evidence-bound, authority-limited, Unknown-preserving, and stop-capable movement. Their difference is what they decide:

- Structure A decides whether bounded material is warranted for lawful reliance.
- Structure B decides whether recovered material may be admitted into a next bounded constitutional state.

Therefore the handoff exists only at the narrow seam where a recovered answer or warrant-preserving artifact becomes an input to a local admissibility test. The repository does not support collapsing the grammars, promoting overlap into identity, or creating a larger handoff architecture.

- Whether the seam is constitutional reality in all domains or a recurring recovery-methodology discipline in some domains remains partly unknown.
- Whether future implementation will expose an admissibility-related diagnostic surface remains unknown and is not authorized here.
- Whether there are additional seam participants beyond recovered answer, bounded artifact, warrant profile, and local admissibility remains unsupported by this bounded review.

Confidence is **low** that repository evidence supports a larger constitutional handoff structure, new office, engine, workflow, registry, planner, scheduler, ownership, runtime architecture, or implementation.
