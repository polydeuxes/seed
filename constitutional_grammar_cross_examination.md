## Method

Each pair is examined independently using the same questions:

1. constitutional dependency;
2. artifact exchange;
3. responsibility identity or distinction;
4. presentation-level similarity;
5. evidence requiring distinction.

Where evidence is absent, the answer is preserved as Unknown or No rather than inferred from symmetry.

# Pairwise examinations

## 1. Question Grammar ↔ Relationship Grammar

### 1. Does either grammar constitutionally depend upon the other?

**Question Grammar depends on relationship evidence only locally, not on Relationship Grammar as a whole neighborhood.**

Question Grammar's topology says it depends on bounded subject identity, admissible evidence, warrant, recurrence, prior question dependencies, explicit authority and negative authority, Unknown preservation, confidence calibration, and stop grammar. It also requires endpoint identity before topology or relationship testing. That means a particular admitted inquiry may require named relations or endpoints, but the Question Grammar neighborhood is not constitutionally dependent on the entire Relationship Grammar survey or vocabulary.

**Relationship Grammar depends on admitted or already recovered participants and evidence, not on Question Grammar as a constitutional owner.**

Relationship Grammar depends on already recovered bounded constitutional participants, repository evidence, provenance, recurrence, local authority, exact identity where required, negative authority, Unknown preservation, consumer-local boundaries, and output boundaries. Question Grammar can admit an inquiry that produces or examines relationship evidence, but the recovered Relationship Grammar is characterized as connective work between already recovered participants rather than as a product owned by Question Grammar.

**Result:** local dependency can exist at inquiry time, but no constitutional dependency of either mature grammar neighborhood on the other is proven.

### 2. Does either grammar produce artifacts consumed by the other?

**Question Grammar may produce admitted inquiry artifacts that Relationship Grammar can later use as bounded evidence.**

Question Grammar artifacts include a bounded question statement, scope and non-goals, evidence corpus boundary, question shape, dependency record, survey warrant relation, admissibility classification, deferred pressure classification, Unknown list, stop reason, negative-authority boundary, artifact expectation, confidence calibration, and handoff to Orientation.

Relationship Grammar consumes permitted bounded material only under warrant or admission profiles. Therefore a Question Grammar artifact may be consumed by a relationship inquiry as evidence of scope, dependency, warrant, refusal, admission, or stop limits.

**Relationship Grammar may produce relationship vocabulary and preservation profiles consumed by future Question Grammar when a question asks about relationships.**

Relationship Grammar artifacts include relationship entries, smallest supported statements, evidence posture, confidence, purpose, authority, boundaries, preservation profiles, negative authority, stop conditions, and Unknowns. Question Grammar may use such artifacts when a possible question requires relation identity, endpoint identity, or relationship-testing authority.

**Result:** artifact exchange is supported only as bounded evidence consumption. It is not automatic, universal, bidirectional runtime flow, ownership, or collapse.

### 3. Are any recovered responsibilities actually the same constitutional responsibility?

**No.**

Question Grammar's responsibility is admission discipline for possible questions becoming lawful bounded constitutional inquiries. Relationship Grammar's responsibility is connective discipline between already recovered constitutional participants. Admission of an inquiry is not the same constitutional responsibility as characterizing support, constraint, authorization, admission, reliance, preservation, refusal, selection, projection, reconciliation, consumption, or emission between participants.

The word `admits` appears in both neighborhoods, but it is not enough to collapse them. In Question Grammar, admission is the question becoming a lawful inquiry or not. In Relationship Grammar, `admits` is a local relationship through a competent boundary after checks. Same presentation word; different bounded constitutional responsibility.

### 4. Are any apparent similarities presentation-level rather than constitutional?

**Yes.**

Shared vocabulary includes evidence, warrant, admission, authority, negative authority, Unknown preservation, confidence, stop, boundary, support, and refusal. The reviewed topology shows these as recurring constitutional discipline across many artifacts, but shared terms do not make the grammars identical.

The strongest presentation-level similarity is `admission`: Question Grammar admits inquiries; Relationship Grammar includes admission as one connective relationship among participants. Repository evidence requires local role, subject, and boundary before treating the similarity as constitutional identity.

### 5. Does repository evidence require these grammars to remain distinct?

**Yes.**

Question Grammar explicitly is not the inquiry itself, Orientation, Recovery, implementation recovery, ownership recovery, topology, responsibility assignment, or participant invention. Relationship Grammar explicitly does not recover implementation, ownership, workflow, universal movement pipeline, object model, state machine, Relationship Engine, universal sequencing, or transitive closure; it also says relationships are not participants.

The reviewed topology preserves Question Grammar as admission-before-orientation discipline and Relationship Grammar as connective work between already recovered participants. Those boundaries require distinction.

### Constitutional road between the pair

**Road supported:** bounded artifact-evidence road.

A Question Grammar artifact can support later relationship examination when it supplies bounded identity, evidence posture, authority limits, dependency, Unknowns, or stop conditions. Relationship Grammar artifacts can support later question admission when a possible question needs relationship identity, endpoint identity, or known connective vocabulary.

**Road type:** presently **independent with local directional uses**. Evidence supports local directional consumption in either direction when a bounded inquiry requires it, but not a standing bidirectional pipeline.

**Ownership compression or collapse:** No.

**Confidence:** High for distinction; medium for local artifact exchange; low for any stronger dependency or district-level flow.

## 2. Question Grammar ↔ External Grammar

### 1. Does either grammar constitutionally depend upon the other?

**Question Grammar does not constitutionally depend on External Grammar as a neighborhood.**

Question Grammar depends on bounded subject identity, evidence posture, warrant, dependencies, authority boundaries, Unknown preservation, confidence, and stop grammar. External representations may be part of an evidence corpus only when the question's admissible evidence permits them. That is local evidence dependency, not a constitutional dependency on External Grammar.

**External Grammar does not constitutionally depend on Question Grammar as a neighborhood, but it depends on bounded family-local recovery conditions that may be established by an admitted inquiry.**

External Grammar depends on a bounded external representation family under review, family-local parsing/decoding/matching/extraction, Seed-owned bounded structures to receive translated evidence, provenance and metadata preservation, negative authority, read-only/non-mutating boundaries where applicable, local translation discipline, Unknown preservation, and family-local confidence.

An admitted question can create the lawful investigation boundary for external recovery, but External Grammar's constitutional responsibility remains representation-to-bounded-structure discipline rather than question admission.

**Result:** no neighborhood-level constitutional dependency is proven.

### 2. Does either grammar produce artifacts consumed by the other?

**Question Grammar can produce scope, non-goals, evidence boundary, negative authority, and artifact expectations that constrain an External Grammar inquiry.**

External Grammar recovery must know which external representation family is under review and which over-promotions are forbidden. Question Grammar artifacts can supply that bounded investigation posture.

**External Grammar can produce bounded Seed-owned structures, provenance, negative-authority statements, and Unknowns that may become evidence for future questions.**

External Grammar artifacts include inquiry note records and orientation views from operator prose, package records and package observations from dpkg text, decoded Prometheus samples and observation shapes, canonical observations, relationship facts from Python source/AST and documentation metadata, repository artifact facts, structural evidence records, negative-authority statements, non-mutation boundaries, and typed Unknowns. Future Question Grammar can treat those as admissible evidence only within their preserved limits.

**Result:** artifact exchange is supported as bounded evidence and scoping. It is not proof that questions parse external representations or that external recovery admits questions.

### 3. Are any recovered responsibilities actually the same constitutional responsibility?

**No.**

Question Grammar decides whether a possible question may become a lawful inquiry. External Grammar distinguishes external representation from recovered bounded Seed-owned structure through family-local translation and negative authority.

Both preserve Unknowns, negative authority, confidence, and stop/refusal boundaries. Those are shared constitutional disciplines, not the same constitutional responsibility.

### 4. Are any apparent similarities presentation-level rather than constitutional?

**Yes.**

Both neighborhoods handle language-like material in some cases. Question Grammar handles questions, prompts, pressures, and inquiry admission. External Grammar handles operator prose, provider syntax, Python source/AST, and documentation metadata as external representations. The similarity that both may encounter prose is presentation-level unless repository evidence shows the same constitutional work.

Question Grammar does not parse operator language into intent or command. External Grammar also refuses operator prose as fact, goal, requirement, command, authorization, intent, ownership, recommendation, next safe move, or architecture. That shared refusal is constitutionally compatible but does not make the neighborhoods identical.

### 5. Does repository evidence require these grammars to remain distinct?

**Yes.**

Question Grammar is not natural-language semantic routing, universal question object, planner, router, scheduler, or workflow. External Grammar is not parser authority, semantic truth, universal expression coverage, universal testimony, workflow, ownership, implementation owner, or architecture by itself.

Question Grammar admits inquiries. External Grammar recovers bounded structures from external representations. Repository evidence requires these to remain distinct.

### Constitutional road between the pair

**Road supported:** bounded inquiry-to-evidence road.

Question Grammar can lawfully bound an inquiry that reviews an external representation family. External Grammar can then produce bounded recovered structures and Unknowns that future questions may use as evidence.

**Road type:** presently **directional by local phase** when such an inquiry occurs: question admission may precede external recovery; recovered external artifacts may later feed future question admission as evidence. The repository does not prove a universal bidirectional road.

**Ownership compression or collapse:** No.

**Confidence:** High for distinction; medium-high for bounded artifact exchange; low for any claim that Question Grammar and External Grammar are one language grammar.
