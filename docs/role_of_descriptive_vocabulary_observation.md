# Role Of Descriptive Vocabulary Observation

## Status

Exploratory observation only.

This document investigates the repository question:

```text
If promotion is not the primary outcome,
what is the role of descriptive vocabulary?
```

It is investigation only. It does not modify implementation, ontology, Inquiry
Orientation, State Summary, runtime behavior, projection behavior, catalogs,
validation, tests, policy, or repository primitives. It does not introduce a
promotion process, create authority policy, or promote any concept named here.

Repository authority wins over this observation. Existing canonical architecture,
ontology, reconciliations, implementation, catalogs, tests, State Summary, and
Inquiry Orientation keep their established authority.

## Question

The narrower descriptive-vs-architectural investigation suggested a contrast:

```text
descriptive vocabulary explains
architectural vocabulary participates
```

The promotion-and-reconciliation investigation then complicated that contrast:
promotion appeared to be only one possible outcome. Other observed outcomes
included suppression, decomposition, narrower substitute, bounded architectural
participation, and continued descriptive use.

This document therefore asks:

```text
What work does descriptive vocabulary perform?
What repository value does it provide?
Why does it repeatedly appear even when it is not promoted?
Can descriptive vocabulary be complete in its own role?
```

The investigation does not assume that descriptive vocabulary is
architecture-in-waiting. It also does not assume that descriptive vocabulary is
stable, final, successful, or separate from architecture in every case.

## Historical descriptive examples

### Learning

`learning` repeatedly appears as a useful way to talk about change in represented
understanding, support, caveats, contradiction visibility, and interpretation.
The learning-as-lens observation uses the term to collect related pressures, but
it also decomposes the work into more specific repository mechanisms rather than
making `learning` a new primitive.

The work performed by the term appears to be explanatory and comparative. It
helps readers notice that several changes all feel learning-like while still
asking which lower-level support path changed. Repository activity becomes
easier because a broad human term can guide investigation before a narrower
claim, support, or lifecycle boundary is selected.

Its usefulness did not depend on promotion. In the reviewed evidence, promotion
would risk hiding the very decomposition the term made visible.

### Understanding

`understanding` is less cleanly descriptive. It appears in observations about
navigation, decompression, claims, operator surfaces, and activation. It helps
name a human-facing difference between having information and being able to use,
explain, continue, or apply it.

The problem it helps solve is not only a missing object problem. It helps expose
failures where knowledge exists but the relevant meaning, route, pressure, or
boundary is not active for a participant. That makes investigation easier: a
reader can ask whether the failure is evidence absence, support weakness,
visibility weakness, navigation weakness, weak decompression, or failed
activation.

Its usefulness only partly depends on promotion. Some understanding-adjacent
questions pressure architectural surfaces such as support, explanation, source
navigation, and State Summary, but the broad word remains useful even where it
is not a field, event kind, projection object, or catalog member.

### Seeing

`seeing` appears mostly as ordinary descriptive language for what an observation,
lens, view, decompression, or navigation surface makes visible. It helps express
the difference between information existing and a participant noticing the
relevant distinction.

The concept helps solve attention and visibility problems. It does not appear to
need direct runtime or projection authority to perform that work. In the reviewed
evidence, `seeing` is useful precisely because it remains a flexible verb for
reader-facing recognition rather than a repository primitive.

### Inquiry

`inquiry` recurs across bridge, movement, preservation, frontier, and orientation
work. It helps describe pursuit of unresolved understanding, movement from a
starting pressure toward a more usable distinction, and the continuity of an open
line across documents or handoffs.

The problem it helps solve is continuity without premature settlement. Inquiry
language lets repository work preserve an open question, a route of attention,
and a reason for continuation without immediately creating a runtime subsystem,
workflow engine, planner, or ontology object.

Its repeated non-promotion does not by itself show failure. The Inquiry
Orientation probe gives inquiry bounded implementation-adjacent visibility, but
that surface is explicitly read-only and isolated. The broader term continues to
perform descriptive work around movement, bridging, and preservation.

### Unresolvedness

`unresolvedness` names the quality of something remaining open, unsettled, or not
fully reducible to a conclusion. It helps preserve uncertainty without requiring
that every uncertainty become a fact type, status enum, validation error, or
frontier.

The problem it helps solve is premature closure. Repository activity becomes
easier because investigators can name why a document should remain exploratory,
why a question survives a partial answer, and why a pressure should not be
collapsed into implementation readiness.

Its usefulness seems especially independent of promotion. If unresolvedness were
prematurely turned into a strong primitive, it might over-specify the very
openness it was preserving.

## Functions of descriptive vocabulary

The reviewed evidence supports these candidate roles, with uneven strength.
They are roles observed in repository practice, not new repository primitives.

### Explanation

Descriptive vocabulary gives readers a way to explain why scattered evidence
belongs in the same investigation. `learning`, `understanding`, `inquiry`,
`pressure`, and `attention` often make a pattern readable before the repository
knows whether a narrower architectural term is justified.

### Pattern recognition

Broad words can make recurrence visible. A term may appear across unrelated
documents not because it is ready for promotion, but because it helps compare
similar shapes: knowledge present but not usable, continuation possible but not
activated, pressure preserved but not selected, or attention triggered without a
settled target.

### Attention guidance

Descriptive vocabulary can steer investigation toward what should be noticed.
`active edge`, `current work position`, `pressure`, and `attention` often guide a
reader toward currentness, selection, and continuation risks without necessarily
being canonical objects.

### Boundary discovery

Descriptive terms are often useful because they fail cleanly. When `learning`,
`inquiry`, or `attention target` starts to overclaim, the failure reveals a
boundary: support change is not all learning; inquiry is not execution; attention
is not selection; a trigger is not a target.

### Distinction preservation

Descriptive vocabulary can preserve distinctions long enough for better
reconciliation. It allows repository work to say that two things should not be
collapsed even before the final names or participation surfaces are settled.

### Investigation framing

Observation and frontier documents frequently use descriptive terms to frame a
question without authorizing implementation. That framing role is valuable when
the next safe move is audit, observation, reconciliation, or continued evidence
collection rather than code.

### Pressure preservation

Some terms preserve why work still matters. `pressure`, `unresolvedness`,
`frontier`, and `active edge` can carry the reason a question remains alive after
facts, documents, and conclusions have been preserved.

### Continuation and navigation support

Descriptive vocabulary helps a future reader resume work. It marks what was
being pursued, what remained unsettled, what distinction mattered, and what
should not be prematurely promoted.

## Authority investigation

The reviewed evidence supports a cautious distinction between authority types.
This is an observation, not a policy.

### Possible descriptive authority

Descriptive vocabulary may possess limited forms of authority when scoped by a
document:

- **Observation authority**: it can authoritatively report that a pattern was
  observed in the reviewed evidence, within the limits of the observation.
- **Attention authority**: it can tell a reader what the document is asking them
  to notice, compare, or keep open.
- **Framing authority**: it can bound the question under investigation and name
  candidate distinctions without settling ontology.

These authority forms are weaker than implementation or projection authority.
They depend on document type, evidence, claim strength, and repository routing.

### Authority not observed for descriptive vocabulary alone

Descriptive vocabulary alone does not appear to grant:

- implementation authority;
- runtime authority;
- projection authority;
- catalog authority;
- validation authority;
- event authority;
- policy authority;
- execution authority.

A descriptive term can later be routed into one of those surfaces, but the mere
usefulness of the term is not sufficient evidence that it should be.

## Inquiry and unresolvedness as test cases

Inquiry and unresolvedness are useful because they repeatedly appear without
settling into the same kind of architecture as observation, evidence, fact,
relationship, capability, or event.

One explanation is failure to mature: perhaps the repository has not yet found
the right inquiry object, unresolvedness state, or continuation representation.
The existence of inquiry frontiers and an Inquiry Orientation probe keeps that
possibility open.

A second explanation is successful descriptive performance: inquiry names active
or preserved pursuit, while unresolvedness names non-closure. Their value may be
to keep movement and uncertainty visible without forcing every open concern into
a state machine, catalog entry, or projection schema.

A third explanation is mixed status: the broad words remain descriptive while
narrower adjacent surfaces become architectural. Inquiry notes and Inquiry
Orientation may provide bounded read-only participation, while `inquiry` as a
larger vocabulary continues to frame movement, bridge, and preservation.

The evidence does not justify choosing only one explanation.

## Lens and orientation comparison

Lens and orientation show a different pattern. Lens language began with
observation and compression concerns, then accumulated stronger architectural
adjacency through State projections, read projections, context views, State
Summary, source navigation, and view responsibility. Even there, the lens/view
reconciliation cautions that `Lens` is not a canonical runtime primitive or a
formal framework.

Orientation appears increasingly load-bearing around current concern, pressure,
reference point, boundary, active edge, continuation, next safe move, selection
rationale, and validation state. The orientation bundle evidence treats those
elements carefully: frequent co-presence is not proof that the bundle is a
primitive.

Compared with learning, seeing, inquiry, and unresolvedness, lens and orientation
appear closer to architecture because they accumulated participation surfaces or
operator-facing responsibilities. But architectural proximity is not shown to be
inherently preferable. The comparison mainly suggests that concepts move closer
to architecture when they must constrain selection, projection, view
responsibility, continuation, or operator-facing use.

## Alternative models

### Descriptive vocabulary is immature architecture

Supported in some cases. Frontiers sometimes preserve questions that may later
be reconciled into implementation or ontology. Lens and orientation show that
some descriptive language can accumulate participation surfaces.

Not sufficient as a general model. Learning, seeing, and unresolvedness appear to
perform useful work without needing direct promotion.

### Descriptive vocabulary is architecture that failed promotion

Supported in failure cases where a concept overclaimed or collapsed distinctions.
The Prometheus and promotion-readiness evidence shows that some would-be
promotion needs suppression, narrowing, or safer routing.

Not sufficient as a general model. Continued descriptive use can be a positive
outcome when the term preserves uncertainty, comparison, or attention better than
a primitive would.

### Descriptive vocabulary exists only temporarily

Sometimes plausible. Investigation language may be scaffolding that disappears
after reconciliation.

Not always supported. Some terms recur because the repository repeatedly needs
human-readable framing even after narrower architecture exists.

### Descriptive and architectural vocabulary perform different work

Strongly supported as a working model, with caveats. Descriptive vocabulary often
explains, notices, compares, frames, and preserves pressure. Architectural
vocabulary participates in fields, events, projections, catalogs, validation,
runtime behavior, tests, and authority boundaries.

The caveat is that a term can hold mixed status across contexts.

### The distinction itself is artificial

Partly supported. Repository prose, documentation observations, natural language
observation, and explanation surfaces show that language can be evidence-bearing
or architecture-adjacent without fitting a simple binary.

Still, the distinction remains useful as a claim-strength guardrail. It prevents
explanatory usefulness from being mistaken for implementation authority.

### Repository investigations preferentially generate descriptive language

Plausible. Observation and frontier documents are designed to preserve questions,
patterns, and uncertainty. They may naturally produce terms that help readers
notice movement before any settled architecture exists.

This does not make the terms meaningless. It does mean recurrence alone is weak
evidence for promotion.

## Uncertainties

- Whether any stable taxonomy of descriptive roles should exist, or whether that
  would over-formalize a useful investigation practice.
- Whether `inquiry` will remain mostly descriptive, gain more bounded
  architectural participation, or be decomposed into narrower terms.
- Whether `unresolvedness` is best treated as a quality, pressure, status,
  frontier property, or only ordinary prose.
- Whether `attention` and `pressure` are moving toward architecture or mainly
  helping frontiers distinguish triggers, targets, selection, and currentness.
- How much evidence is required before a descriptive term should be considered
  complete in its descriptive role.
- Whether mixed-status terms need clearer repository routing, or whether local
  document scope is enough.

## Non-conclusions

This observation does not conclude that:

- descriptive vocabulary is superior to architectural vocabulary;
- architectural vocabulary is superior to descriptive vocabulary;
- all descriptive vocabulary should remain descriptive;
- all descriptive vocabulary should be promoted;
- descriptive vocabulary should become a new ontology category;
- observation authority, attention authority, or framing authority should become
  new repository primitives;
- inquiry, unresolvedness, pressure, attention, learning, understanding, or
  seeing should be promoted;
- lens or orientation should be treated as fully settled runtime primitives.

The strongest cautious finding is narrower:

```text
Descriptive vocabulary can provide repository value without promotion when it
helps readers notice, compare, preserve uncertainty, frame investigation, keep
pressure visible, or avoid premature architectural commitment.
```

That finding remains exploratory and subordinate to repository authority.
