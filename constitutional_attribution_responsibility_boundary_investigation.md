# Constitutional Attribution / Responsibility Boundary Investigation

## Bounded question

Across recurring repository evidence, when Seed receives or produces a claim that an event, source, account, device, identity, role, system, deployment, vendor, institution, file, fact, or diagnostic finding is attributed to some actor or owner, what prevents Seed from treating that attribution as proof of responsibility, accountability, fault, intent, negligence, legal liability, moral blame, implementation ownership, or implementation pressure without separately preserved actor evidence, authority boundary, conduct evidence, causal support, temporal provenance, role/scope evidence, policy/legal standard where relevant, claim form, support path, confidence limits, Unknowns, and lawful stop?

This investigation performs exactly one bounded constitutional investigation: **Attribution != Responsibility**. It does not implement anything, create a schema, create an attribution or blame engine, perform world research, legal research, moral judgment, security attribution, identity resolution, or evaluate any real actor, product, vendor, incident, policy, law, account, device, IP address, or deployment.

## Attribution analysis

Repository evidence already uses attribution in limited operational ways, chiefly as provenance, association, or diagnostic attachment:

1. A source may be attributed as the origin, reporter, renderer, translator, or external role for a claim.
2. An event may carry actor, workspace, causation, authority-boundary, or provenance fields as preserved event metadata.
3. A diagnostic may associate a finding with an owner candidate, predicate, surface, source, or support path.
4. An implementation audit may name an owner or ownership discrepancy as a bounded implementation-local question.
5. A support surface may say which source, observation, fact support, reasoning path, selection path, or navigation result led to a visible claim.

That evidence supports attribution as **bounded attachment of a claim, record, event, observation, diagnostic finding, or support path to a source-like or owner-like referent under provenance and confidence limits**. It does not support attribution as truth, conduct proof, person identity, responsibility, fault, intent, negligence, legal liability, moral blame, implementation ownership, or implementation pressure.

## Responsibility analysis

Repository evidence already uses `responsibility` mainly in two non-personal senses:

1. **Implementation-local responsibility**: a function, dataclass, report shape, CLI path, cache/build corridor, event corridor, read model, diagnostic payload, or test owns one bounded behavior or handoff.
2. **Constitutional responsibility / lawful movement responsibility**: an artifact or capability must preserve evidence, authority, provenance, boundaries, Unknowns, confidence, and stop conditions.

The repository does not show a general actor-responsibility model. It does not show a liability model, blame model, moral-judgment model, policy-violation engine, negligence engine, intent engine, security-attribution engine, or identity-resolution engine. Therefore `responsibility` in this investigation must remain bounded: it may describe repository-owned implementation work only where implementation evidence shows compressed local responsibility, and it may describe actor responsibility only as an unsupported constitutional pressure unless separately preserved actor, conduct, causal, authority, temporal, claim-form, standard, and support evidence exist.

## Source attribution / truth distinction

Source attribution answers: **who or what reported, supplied, rendered, translated, carried, or preserved this claim?** Truth requires separately preserved evidence support, warrant, authority boundary, claim form, temporal provenance, confidence limits, support path, contradiction handling, and Unknown preservation.

A source-attributed statement may lawfully be preserved as `source S reported claim C` when the source material, source role, provenance, time, rendering/translation boundary, and support path are preserved. It may not become `C is true` merely because a source said it, repeated it, translated it, blamed someone, or appeared authoritative.

## Event attribution / actor responsibility distinction

Event attribution answers: **what event record, log, diagnostic, observation, or ledger entry associated event X with actor/account/device/source Y?** Actor responsibility requires more: identity binding, authority boundary, conduct evidence, causal support, temporal fit, role/scope evidence, affected-subject or impact evidence where relevant, claim form, confidence, and lawful standard where relevant.

An event record can preserve that an actor field, account field, device field, IP field, or source field existed. That does not prove the named actor personally acted, intended, caused harm, violated policy, or bears legal/moral responsibility.

## Account attribution / person attribution distinction

Account attribution ties an action or event to an account identifier, credential, session, user record, or system identity. Person attribution ties conduct to a human or legal actor. Repository boundary evidence around capability/use, evidence/support, identity preservation, and Unknowns prevents account attribution from becoming person attribution without identity-binding evidence.

Before Seed may say a person performed an action, it must preserve how the account maps to that person for the relevant time, scope, credential state, delegation state, compromise/automation possibility, source authority, confidence, contradictions, and Unknowns. Otherwise Seed may only say the event was attributed to the account under preserved source/provenance limits.

## Device attribution / owner responsibility distinction

Device attribution ties an event to a device identifier, host, sensor, filesystem, runtime entity, or emitted signal. Owner responsibility ties a person or institution to responsibility for the event or outcome. Device ownership, deployment, possession, or management does not by itself prove use, conduct, causation, intent, negligence, policy violation, or liability.

Seed may preserve that device D emitted or was associated with event X only when the device identity, observation source, timestamp, event form, confidence, and support path are preserved. Seed may not say the device owner is responsible without separate ownership scope, control, conduct, causation, standard, and temporal evidence.

## IP attribution / user responsibility distinction

IP attribution ties a record to a network identifier. User responsibility requires identity binding and conduct evidence beyond network appearance. Dynamic assignment, NAT, proxies, shared networks, spoofing limits, log scope, collection time, clock drift, source reliability, and confidence all matter as Unknown-preserving boundaries.

Seed may preserve `IP A appeared in log L at time T` if the log source, timestamp, field meaning, collection path, and confidence are preserved. It may not say user U did X, user U is responsible, or user U intended X solely from IP appearance.

## Role access / role action distinction

Role access shows capability, permission, eligibility, or potential authority. Role action shows conduct. The capability/use boundary directly prevents access from becoming use. A role having access to perform X does not prove the role exercised that access, caused an outcome, acted responsibly, violated policy, or intended anything.

A lawful role-action claim needs preserved access evidence plus conduct evidence, event attribution, identity binding, temporal fit, causal support where relevant, and confidence limits. Without conduct evidence, Seed must stop at `role R had access/capability under source S at time T`.

## Ownership candidate / implementation ownership distinction

An ownership candidate is diagnostic or inquiry evidence that a surface, function, predicate, source, or area may be related to an owner. Implementation ownership is recovered only when implementation evidence shows a bounded local responsibility, hidden boundary, affected reasoning/testing/evolution/authority, and a compatibility-preserving recovery path.

The pressure and ownership-discrepancy surfaces can orient inquiry, but they cannot command implementation work or assign implementation ownership by themselves. A candidate remains a candidate until repository evidence supports a concrete owner and boundary.

## Deployment ownership / conduct responsibility distinction

Deployment ownership means a person, team, institution, vendor, or system is associated with deploying, supplying, hosting, operating, configuring, or maintaining a system. Conduct responsibility means the actor is responsible for a particular action, outcome, harm, policy violation, or legal/moral condition.

Deployment ownership may be relevant evidence, but it is not sufficient. Seed would need role/scope evidence, temporal fit, authority boundary, conduct evidence, causal support, policy/legal standard where relevant, affected-subject or impact evidence, confidence, and Unknowns before making a bounded responsibility-related claim.

## System causation / actor responsibility distinction

System causation, if separately supported, concerns the causal role of a system in producing an outcome. Actor responsibility concerns a human, institutional, or legal actor's accountable relation to the conduct or outcome. The causation boundary already prevents association, sequence, source-attributed causation, diagnostic adjacency, and model-inferred causation from becoming causation. Even supported system causation does not automatically prove actor responsibility, intent, negligence, policy violation, or liability.

A system may cause or contribute to outcome X while actor responsibility remains Unknown because actor identity, control, foreseeability, standard, authority, conduct, and temporal scope may be unresolved.

## Actual use / responsible use distinction

Actual use proves only that a capability or system was used under preserved evidence. Responsible use adds a normative or standard-bound judgment: appropriate, authorized, policy-compliant, legally valid, non-negligent, or morally acceptable use. Repository evidence distinguishes capability from use; this investigation extends only the smaller supported non-promotion: actual use is not responsible use.

Responsible-use claims require the use evidence plus the applicable policy/legal/operational standard, authority to apply that standard, scope, actor evidence, temporal provenance, affected-subject evidence where relevant, confidence, and Unknown preservation.

## Responsibility / legal liability distinction

Responsibility may be colloquial, operational, implementation-local, causal, institutional, moral, or legal. Legal liability is a legal conclusion requiring jurisdiction, source-of-law lineage, legal authority, adjudicative authority or appropriate legal process, elements, standards, evidence, burden, defenses, temporal applicability, and lawful decision boundary.

Seed's legal-boundary evidence supports preserving legal-analysis artifacts, source-of-law lineage, warrant, and authority boundaries. It does not support Seed declaring liability. Therefore even responsibility evidence must not become legal liability without a separately supported legal authority and adjudicative boundary.

## Legal authority / legal responsibility distinction

Legal authority identifies a source, actor, or institution's authority to act, decide, interpret, enforce, or rely on law. Legal responsibility identifies that an actor bears legal consequence or duty for conduct/outcome. Authority to perform, authorize, regulate, or analyze does not prove that an actor violated law or bears liability.

Seed may preserve that a legal source or institutional actor has a claimed authority role only under source-of-law/provenance boundaries. It may not convert authority into legal responsibility without legal standard, conduct evidence, causal/impact evidence where relevant, adjudicative authority, and lawful stop.

## Policy permission / policy compliance distinction

Policy permission says a policy allowed, authorized, or did not prohibit a behavior under some interpretation and time. Policy compliance says a specific actor/action satisfied all applicable policy conditions. Permission is not proof of compliance because conditions, scope, temporal applicability, exceptions, implementation facts, actor conduct, and required approvals may remain Unknown.

Seed may preserve policy permission as bounded source material or authority-limited analysis. It may not say policy compliance occurred without the policy text/source, authority boundary, applicable standard, conduct evidence, required approvals, temporal provenance, contradictions, and confidence.

## Policy violation allegation / proven violation distinction

A policy violation allegation is a claim that someone violated a policy. A proven violation requires preserved policy standard, applicable scope and time, actor identity, conduct evidence, standard application authority, support path, confidence, contradiction handling, and lawful decision boundary.

Source-reported allegations, operator notes, repeated narratives, model inferences, account/device/IP attributions, role access, or diagnostic ownership candidates may preserve allegation evidence but may not become proven violation.

## Source-reported blame / independently supported responsibility distinction

Source-reported blame is a source-attributed claim about responsibility, fault, intent, negligence, violation, liability, or moral blame. Independently supported responsibility requires support that does not merely restate the blame narrative: actor evidence, identity binding, conduct evidence, causal support, temporal fit, role/scope evidence, affected-subject evidence where relevant, applicable standard, authority boundary, confidence, and Unknown preservation.

Seed may say `source S blamed actor Y for X` when the source material and provenance are preserved. It may not say `actor Y is responsible for X` merely because S blamed Y.

## Repeated blame narrative / responsibility evidence distinction

Repetition can increase visibility of a narrative but does not create independent support. Repository evidence around repetition/proof, authority/volume, source independence, and narrative/history prevents repeated blame from becoming responsibility evidence unless the repetitions are meaningfully independent support paths with preserved source roles, material basis, temporal provenance, and claim forms.

Multiple sources repeating the same unsupported blame can lawfully support a claim about narrative prevalence, not responsibility.

## Model-inferred responsibility / responsibility evidence distinction

Model output is not evidence. A model may summarize, infer, classify, or speculate that an actor is responsible, but the model's output is not actor evidence, conduct evidence, causal support, legal authority, policy standard, identity binding, or proof. At most, model output may orient inquiry when clearly labeled and bounded by source materials and Unknowns.

Seed must not treat model-inferred responsibility as responsibility evidence.

## Diagnostic ownership candidate / implementation pressure distinction

A diagnostic ownership candidate is a read-only or diagnostic association that may indicate where to inspect. Implementation pressure requires concrete repository evidence: behaviorally real responsibility plurality compressed in one owner/corridor, hidden boundary, consequences for reasoning/testing/evolution/authority, and a lawful compatibility-preserving slice. Diagnostic candidates may be evidence for inquiry, not commands to implement.

The app-visible pressure audit confirms this orientation role: it reports categories, scores, reasons, and recommended inspection commands, not implementation authority or owner assignment.

## Responsibility evidence / intent distinction

Responsibility evidence, even if bounded, does not prove intent. Intent requires separate evidence of mental state, purpose, knowledge, objective, communicated plan, instruction, or legally/operationally recognized intent standard under authority to apply that standard. Conduct, access, attribution, causation, outcome, harm, or responsibility indicators do not automatically preserve intent.

Seed may preserve intent allegations or source-attributed intent claims only as claim-form-bounded evidence unless independently supported.

## Responsibility evidence / negligence distinction

Negligence is not responsibility in general. It usually requires a standard or duty, breach, causation, harm/impact, scope, temporal applicability, and authority to apply the standard. Repository evidence supports no negligence engine. Therefore responsibility evidence, attribution, use, capability, system causation, policy permission, or source blame cannot become negligence without a separately preserved standard, conduct, causal/impact evidence, legal/policy boundary, and lawful decision authority.

## Actor-evidence analysis

Actor evidence identifies the actor at the resolution needed by the claim: person, account, device, system, role, vendor, institution, file, source, or diagnostic owner. It prevents drift from `account`, `device`, `IP`, `role`, or `source` to `person`, `owner`, `institution`, or `responsible actor`. If actor resolution is insufficient, the actor portion remains Unknown.

## Identity-binding analysis

Identity binding explains why one identifier may lawfully stand for another in the claim: account-to-person, device-to-owner, IP-to-user, role-to-operator, vendor-to-product, institution-to-deployment, file-to-maintainer, or diagnostic-to-owner. Binding must preserve source, time, scope, confidence, contradictions, and Unknowns. Without binding, Seed stops at the narrower identifier.

## Access-evidence analysis

Access evidence shows permission, capability, eligibility, or technical possibility. It is relevant to opportunity or scope but does not prove action. Access evidence can support bounded claims such as `role R had access to capability C at time T`, but action requires conduct evidence.

## Conduct-evidence analysis

Conduct evidence supports that an actor performed an action. It may include event records, observations, corroborated logs, commands, approvals, commits, messages, or other preserved source material. Conduct evidence must be attached to claim form, actor resolution, time, authority, support path, confidence, and Unknowns. Conduct evidence is necessary before Seed may say `actor Y performed action X`.

## Causal-support analysis

Causal support is required when the claim says the actor, account, device, system, policy, deployment, or action caused an outcome or harm. Repository causation evidence refuses to promote sequence, adjacency, source-attributed causation, diagnostic adjacency, log adjacency, co-presence, actual use, or harm into causation. Responsibility claims that include outcome responsibility therefore need separately preserved causal support.

## Affected-subject / impact-evidence analysis

Where responsibility-related claims concern harm, impact, affected subjects, policy injury, operational effect, legal consequence, or moral blame, Seed must preserve affected-subject or impact evidence. Risk indicators, possible harm, claimed harm, or source blame do not prove impact.

## Temporal-provenance analysis

Temporal provenance anchors the claim to the relevant time: source publication, observation time, event time, account/session time, device/IP assignment time, policy effective time, deployment time, conduct time, impact time, and review time. Without temporal fit, Seed cannot lawfully bridge attribution to action or responsibility.

## Policy / legal-standard analysis where relevant

Policy, liability, negligence, legal responsibility, and legal violation claims require the applicable standard and authority boundary. Seed may preserve policy/legal source material and analysis artifacts, but it cannot adjudicate legal liability, policy violation, negligence, or intent without repository-supported authority and standards. Where the standard is absent, inapplicable, unresolved, or outside Seed authority, the lawful result is Unknown or source-attributed allegation only.

## Claim-form analysis

Claim form controls lawful movement. `Source S reports Y`, `log L attributes event X to account A`, `device D emitted event X`, `role R had access`, `actor Y performed action X`, `actor Y is responsible`, `actor Y violated policy`, and `actor Y is legally liable` are different claims with different evidence requirements. Seed must preserve the claim form and may not silently strengthen it.

## Confidence / evidence-strength analysis

Confidence and evidence strength limit reliance. Strong attribution evidence can support a strong attribution claim while still supporting no responsibility claim. Confidence does not expand authority, replace missing standards, resolve Unknowns, or turn source attribution into truth.

## Unknown preservation

Unknown preservation is the primary constitutional fuse. If actor identity, identity binding, access scope, conduct, causation, impact, time, source authority, legal/policy standard, claim form, support path, confidence, or authority boundary is missing or unresolved, Seed must preserve the Unknown rather than promote attribution into responsibility, liability, fault, blame, intent, negligence, policy violation, legal responsibility, moral responsibility, implementation ownership, or implementation pressure.

## Lawful bounded uses of attribution evidence

Attribution evidence may lawfully support bounded claims when the evidence matches the claim form:

1. `Source S reported claim C` when source material, role, provenance, time, rendering/translation boundary, and support path are preserved.
2. `Event X was attributed to account/device/IP/source Y in record R` when record identity, field meaning, event time, collection path, confidence, and Unknowns are preserved.
3. `Account A was associated with action X in system S` when account identity, system source, event record, time, scope, and confidence are preserved.
4. `Role R had access to capability C` when policy/configuration/source, time, scope, and confidence are preserved.
5. `Diagnostic D identified owner candidate O for finding F` when diagnostic surface, finding, candidate basis, read-only scope, record scope, mutation boundary, confidence, and Unknowns are preserved.
6. `Deployment or ownership evidence is relevant to inquiry Q` when it remains inquiry pressure and does not become conduct responsibility or implementation pressure.

## Preservation requirements before selected claims

### Before Seed may say `event X was attributed to actor/account/device/source Y`

Seed must preserve event identity, source or record identity, field semantics, attribution target at the exact supported resolution, event time and collection time, provenance, support path, source role/authority boundary, confidence/evidence strength, contradictions, and Unknowns.

### Before Seed may say `actor Y performed action X`

Seed must additionally preserve actor evidence at the required resolution, identity binding where an account/device/IP/role/source stands for the actor, conduct evidence, temporal fit, access/scope evidence where relevant, support path, confidence, contradiction handling, and Unknowns. Attribution alone is insufficient.

### Before Seed may say `actor Y is responsible for X`

Seed must additionally preserve the responsibility claim form, role/scope boundary, conduct evidence, causal support where outcome responsibility is claimed, affected-subject or impact evidence where relevant, source authority, applicable policy/operational/legal standard if the responsibility is standard-bound, confidence, and lawful authority to make that bounded claim. If these are not present, Seed may preserve only attribution, allegation, inquiry pressure, or Unknown.

### Before Seed may say `actor Y is legally liable`, `actor Y violated policy`, `actor Y acted negligently`, or `actor Y intended X`

Seed must preserve all actor/action/responsibility requirements plus the applicable legal or policy source, source-of-law or policy lineage, temporal applicability, jurisdiction/scope, elements or criteria, authority to apply the standard, evidentiary support for each element, burden/standard if relevant, defenses or contrary evidence where relevant, intent or negligence-specific evidence where claimed, and lawful adjudicative or policy decision boundary. Repository evidence currently does not support Seed making these conclusions as its own determinations.

## Lawful stop

The lawful stop condition is:

> If the preserved evidence supports only attribution, allegation, access, deployment, source report, diagnostic ownership candidate, system causation, actual use, or responsibility pressure, and separately preserved actor evidence, identity binding, conduct evidence, causal support, temporal provenance, role/scope evidence, affected-subject or impact evidence where relevant, policy/legal standard where relevant, claim form, support path, confidence limits, Unknowns, and authority boundary are missing or insufficient, Seed must stop at the narrower preserved claim and mark the stronger responsibility, liability, fault, blame, intent, negligence, policy violation, legal responsibility, moral responsibility, implementation ownership, or implementation pressure claim as Unknown or unsupported.

## Supported conclusions

- Attribution is a bounded provenance/association/attachment condition, not truth or responsibility.
- Responsibility vocabulary in the repository is primarily implementation-local or constitutional-preservation vocabulary, not a general actor-responsibility model.
- Source attribution, event attribution, account attribution, device attribution, IP attribution, actor attribution, ownership attribution, deployment attribution, source-reported blame, and diagnostic ownership candidates must not automatically become responsibility, accountability, liability, fault, blame, intent, negligence, policy violation, legal responsibility, moral responsibility, implementation ownership, or implementation pressure.
- The controlling fuse is claim-form discipline plus preserved evidence, authority boundary, temporal provenance, support path, confidence limits, and Unknown preservation.
- Attribution evidence can support narrow attribution claims and inquiry orientation when properly preserved.

## Unsupported conclusions

- That Seed already has or needs a responsibility schema.
- That Seed already has or needs a liability, blame, negligence, intent, policy-violation, legal-liability, security-attribution, identity-resolution, source-scoring, authority-scoring, credibility-scoring, artifact-scoring, root-cause, or truth engine.
- That account/device/IP attribution proves a person acted.
- That access proves action.
- That ownership candidate proves implementation ownership.
- That deployment ownership proves conduct responsibility.
- That system causation proves actor responsibility.
- That actual use proves responsible use.
- That responsibility proves legal liability, policy violation, intent, or negligence.
- That source-reported or repeated blame is independently supported responsibility.
- That model-inferred responsibility is evidence.
- That this investigation creates implementation pressure.

## Implementation recommendation

No implementation work is recommended. The repository evidence supports a constitutional non-promotion boundary only: **Attribution != Responsibility**. Future work should remain stopped unless a concrete repository-visible failure shows that an existing surface is actually promoting attribution, allegation, account/device/IP association, role access, deployment ownership, source blame, model inference, or diagnostic ownership candidate into responsibility, liability, fault, blame, intent, negligence, policy violation, legal responsibility, moral responsibility, implementation ownership, or implementation pressure.
